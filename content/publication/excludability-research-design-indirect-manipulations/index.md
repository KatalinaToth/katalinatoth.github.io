abstract: "When putative causes cannot be directly manipulated (e.g., psychological states), experimental researchers must resort to encouragements (i.e., manipulations that increase the likelihood that subjects respond in the intended manner). When analyzing the effect of an encouraged variable, scholars must assume that this variable is the sole pathway through which the encouragement affects the outcome (the exclusion restriction). Few papers address the possibility that another intermediary variable may transmit the encouragement's influence to the dependent variable. We propose a three-step approach for making the exclusion restriction more defensible. This approach uses theoretical arguments to design encouragements that are then tested iteratively using (1) manipulation checks and (2) estimation of 'first-stage' effects of the encouragements on posited intermediary variables. Although no method can ensure that exclusion restrictions are satisfied, ours leverages researchers' theoretical intuitions about causal pathways and multiple encouragements to make core assumptions as credible as possible."
